The Great Gatsby

by Francis Scott Key Fitzgerald

The central figure of The Great Gatsby is Jay Gatsby, a rich man living in West Egg and throwing lavish parties with the hope of gaining the respect of Daisy Buchanan. Even though Nick Carraway is the narrator, the novel revolves around Gatsby's dream and eventual failure. Gatsby is portrayed as optimistic and mysterious. In Chapter 1, Nick sees him reaching for "a single green light, minute and far away," which symbolizes his dream for Daisy and the life he desires. This reveals not only his hope but also the mismatch between desire and reality.Gatsby's obsessive love for Daisy and his passion to re-create the past render him the emotional center of the novel. Through him, the novel teaches us how individuals are blinded by illusions that never take place. He is both the tragedy and the beauty of the American Dream. The central conflict of Gatsby is his desire to recreate the past when it cannot be recreated. He has the illusion that if he becomes rich, then Daisy would love him as she loved him in the past. In Chapter 6, he declares, "Can't repeat the past? … Why of course you can!" demonstrating how deeply he lives in his world of fantasy. His conflict is within himself, hope vs. reality: he wants to revive a love affair that's already over. Outside, he also conflicts with Tom Buchanan, the husband of Daisy, who mocks Gatsby for not being from a wealthy, old-money background. When Daisy will not let Tom go, Gatsby's fantasy begins to break, and he realizes that love cannot be bought with money. His conflict between his desire and reality is the reason for his own death. With Gatsby's failure, the novel warns us that holding too close to fantasies will ruin a person. The climax of the novel occurs in Chapter 7, as Gatsby, Daisy, and Tom confront each other in a fiery, heated exchange at the Plaza Hotel. The intensity of the scene is matched by the physical heat. Daisy's revelation "I did love him once, but I loved you too" shatters Gatsby's illusion of possessing her wholly. By this time, he is aware that his dream vision of Daisy never existed. This is a turning point in the novel because it takes away Gatsby's last hope. In short time thereafter, tragedy befalls in the form of Daisy driving Gatsby's car and accidentally killing Myrtle Wilson. Gatsby is blamed despite all this, and he loses everything his dream, his love, and his peace of mind. This peak illustrates how the deceptions of Gatsby, grounded in illusory hopes, crumble before the savagery of reality. The setting of The Great Gatsby also adds to the themes and significance of the novel. Situated in New York during the 1920s, the novel portrays an age of plenty, change, and moral decay. Gatsby's West Egg mansion is symbolic of new money and aspirations of individuals to rise higher in society, while East Egg home of Daisy and Tom symbolizes old money, tradition, and authority. Across them lies the Valley of Ashes, a desolate wasteland that symbolizes the emptiness under wealth and the deterioration of the American Dream (Chapter 2). The green light at the end of Daisy's dock, also a part of the physical setting, is symbolic of Gatsby's unreachable dreams (Chapter 5). Fitzgerald uses these contrasting places to highlight the social separations and delusions that dominate the characters' lives. The environment forces readers to feel the contrast between Gatsby's dream and the bitter reality of things. The overarching theme of The Great Gatsby is that dreams are not only beautiful, but also ruinous. Gatsby's pursuit of Daisy is also an extension of the larger American Dream that success and happiness are available to all if one works hard enough. Gatsby's dream is built on falsehood, though, rather than reality. In the final chapter, Nick reflects, "Gatsby believed in the green light, the orgastic future that year by year recedes before us," and that people always reach for something that is just out of their grasp. The novel teaches that dreams based on deceit or nostalgia cannot last. It also demonstrates that riches and status do not bring true happiness or love. Gatsby's failure proves that it is a waste to pursue an impossible dream. Through the application of this topic, Fitzgerald reminds readers that true fulfillment comes from accepting reality rather than fantasies. The entirety of the elements of the book characters, plot, setting, and theme supports this thesis. Gatsby seems mysterious and promising initially and Nick admires his persistence.As the book goes on, Nick discovers what has been done with Gatsby's past and bears witness to the narcissism of Daisy and Tom. Action moves away from the wild parties of West Egg (Chapters 3–4) to the somber aftermath of Myrtle's murder (Chapter 7). Settings also change from Gatsby's rich, full house to his quiet, empty pool where he dies (Chapter 8).
